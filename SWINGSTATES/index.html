why states swing

by Leila Thompson

Every four years in the United States Presidential election there are a class of states that are anticipated to be "swing" states because they could go wither way, blue or red. This often makes them the focus of intense campaigning and tension on election day, but what actually makes a state swing?

Both the republican and democratic party have key demographics that make up each party; People who vote republican tend to be older, white and male while people who vote democratic tend to be younger, minorities, and female.

The demographics of swing states, much like their political results, tend to be neither one or the other; they are diverse states with a range of races represented in their non-white population and have predominately middle aged residents

While all the swing states' population are mostly white, all of them also have a diverse amount of other races represented in their non-white population.

non-white population by state

When it comes to how these demographics manifest themselves politically, over 60% of elections since 2000 have resulted in a republican win

But, of the 174 congressional districts represented and 24 senate seats, less than 50% have stayed with the same party for the past 20 years.

Presidential elections have had the most "swing" with 75% of the twelve 2020 swing states changing party at least once since 2000
